II THE CASES
CASE #004 SUMMER 2025

NYC Department of Finance

Data Science Graduate Intern

The city's property records were a mess. Somebody had to make sense of the spatial data. He built Python Dash applications—interactive, precise—for valuation modelling of high-end properties. Dashboards for the brass showing tax-to-income ratios, borough by borough, quantile by quantile. Then he wired up CI/CD pipelines through GitHub Actions, automating the extraction from ESRI's geospatial API. Clean. Efficient. Nobody asked questions.

CASE #003 2023–2024

World Bank — LAC Chief Economist

Research Analyst

Eleven countries. Eleven household surveys. The kind of job where you lose track of which timezone you're in. He built the R Shiny application that computed non-salary labor costs across all of them. Synthesized labor and tax regulations into spreadsheets sharp enough to cut. Co-wrote the LCRCE flagship report on competition, trade, and antitrust—the one that landed on every desk in the building. Compiled over a thousand research papers into systematic reviews. Nobody said thank you. Nobody ever does.

CASE #002 2022–2023

UNDP — Economic Researcher

SDG & Development Policy

Twenty-five nations in the portfolio. He built the R Shiny app that generated country reports on demand—real-time queries, macroeconomic data, human development indicators. Then COVID-19 hit the surveys. Twenty-two phone surveys, high-frequency, tracking household welfare as the world fell apart. He led the dissemination. Co-authored the UNDP-IDB report on migration—nine RCTs, one shared truth about a better world.

CASE #001 2020–2022

UNDP — Junior Economist

SDG & Development Policy (Remote)

The early days. Remote. Alone with the data. Seventeen Latinobarómetro surveys on inequality, fiscal fairness, redistribution—the questions nobody wants honest answers to. He co-wrote Chapter 4 of the Regional Human Development Report: "Trapped." Violence and inequality, tangled like old wiring. Then twenty-four more phone surveys. Stata, R, VBA—whatever tool fit the job. The policymakers needed numbers. He gave them numbers.
